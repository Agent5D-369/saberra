# Saberra™ Dream Target Clients

**Seberra™** Dream Target Clients

## Segment 1: Notion-native distributed teams

**Priority:** Highest.

These are progressive consultancies, remote agencies, B Corps, social enterprises, DAOs-in-transition, regenerative organizations, and mission-driven companies already running on Google Workspace and Notion.

**Why ideal:**

They already believe in structured documentation, but they are drowning in meeting context. They are big enough to feel knowledge bleed, but not so large that procurement kills the sale.

**Buyer:** Founder, COO, Head of Ops, Chief of Staff, Executive Director.

**Sales cycle:** 2–6 weeks.

**Risk:** If they are not already disciplined enough to review AI-extracted items, the system becomes another graveyard.

## Segment 2: Founder-led agencies and consultancies

**Why ideal:**

Client decisions, deliverables, risks, and tasks often live across calls, Slack, docs, and founder memory. The pain of losing context is immediate and expensive.

**Sales cycle:** 1–4 weeks.

**Risk:** They may compare it to cheap note-takers unless the positioning is elevated.

## Segment 3: Nonprofits and NGOs with complex stakeholder memory

**Why ideal:**

They have board meetings, donors, programs, governance records, policy decisions, grants, and frequent staff turnover.

**Sales cycle:** 1–4 months.

**Risk:** Budget sensitivity and slow approval.

## Segment 4: Teal, sociocratic, holacratic, and cooperative organizations

**Why ideal:**

The system was built around governance circles, roles, canon changes, and distributed decision-making. That is a rare differentiator.

**Sales cycle:** 2–8 weeks if founder-led, longer if board-governed.

**Risk:** Some may love the philosophy but lack operational discipline or budget.

## Segment 5: Growing startups with high onboarding drag

**Why ideal:**

New hires asking “where is this documented?” is a buying trigger. Knowledge bleed becomes painful after 15–30 people.

**Sales cycle:** 2–8 weeks.

**Risk:** They may prefer existing venture-backed enterprise tools like Glean once they are larger.

# Phrases Dream Targets Are Saying

## 1. “Where did that decision go?”

These are the people losing decisions in meetings.

- “Didn’t we already decide this?”
- “Why are we reopening this conversation again?”
- “Where is the decision from that meeting?”
- “Who was supposed to write that down?”
- “I remember us talking about it, but I don’t remember what we landed on.”
- “We keep making the same decision over and over.”
- “Nobody knows what was actually agreed to.”
- “The meeting happened, but the decision disappeared.”
- “I need the context behind why we chose this.”
- “We need a source of truth for decisions.”

**Marketing translation:**

Your team does not have a meeting problem. It has a decision-memory problem.

---

## 2. “Everything is in someone’s head.”

This is the founder bottleneck / key-person risk pain.

- “Too much of this lives in my head.”
- “If Sarah leaves, we’re screwed.”
- “Only one person knows how this works.”
- “I’m tired of being the company search engine.”
- “Everyone comes to me for context.”
- “We can’t scale if every answer has to come through me.”
- “The founder is still the operating system.”
- “Our processes are documented, but the real context isn’t.”
- “We have docs, but the judgment calls are in people’s heads.”
- “When someone leaves, they take half the company with them.”

**Marketing translation:**

Stop letting your most important knowledge walk out the door.

---

## 3. “Our documentation is always behind.”

This is the knowledge-base decay problem.

- “The docs are outdated.”
- “Nobody updates Notion.”
- “We wrote that process six months ago, but it’s not true anymore.”
- “The documentation never keeps up with the work.”
- “We have a knowledge base, but nobody trusts it.”
- “The real answer is usually in Slack or a meeting recording.”
- “Notion is full of half-finished pages.”
- “We document things after the fact, if we remember.”
- “We need documentation that updates as work happens.”
- “Our knowledge base is more like a junk drawer.”

**Marketing translation:**

Documentation fails when humans have to remember to create it.

---

## 4. “New hires take forever to get up to speed.”

This is onboarding drag.

- “It takes months for new people to understand how we work.”
- “Every new hire asks the same questions.”
- “We don’t have a good way to transfer context.”
- “They know the task, but not the history.”
- “Onboarding is too dependent on whoever trains them.”
- “New people don’t know why things are the way they are.”
- “We need them productive faster.”
- “They’re missing the invisible context.”
- “The org has too much tribal knowledge.”
- “We need a way for new people to ask the company what happened before they arrived.”

**Marketing translation:**

Give new hires the context of a tenured team member in days, not months.

---

## 5. “Meeting notes are not enough.”

This is where they know AI note-takers are insufficient.

- “The summary is nice, but now what?”
- “We have meeting notes, but nothing turns into action.”
- “Fireflies gives us transcripts, but it doesn’t create memory.”
- “We still have to manually move everything into Notion.”
- “The notes exist, but nobody uses them.”
- “Meeting summaries don’t tell us what changed.”
- “A transcript is not a system.”
- “We need the action items, decisions, and risks extracted automatically.”
- “The meeting bot captured it, but the organization didn’t learn from it.”
- “We don’t need more notes. We need memory.”

**Marketing translation:**

Meeting summaries capture a call. Living memory captures the organization.

---

## 6. “We can’t find anything.”

This is search/retrieval pain.

- “Where is that document?”
- “Which meeting was that in?”
- “Was that in Slack, email, Notion, or Drive?”
- “I know we talked about this somewhere.”
- “Search is useless if you don’t know the exact word.”
- “I spend too much time hunting for context.”
- “We have too many places where knowledge lives.”
- “I need one place to ask what happened.”
- “Why is finding internal information this hard?”
- “I wish I could just ask the organization.”

**Marketing translation:**

Ask your organization what it already knows.

---

## 7. “Tasks disappear after meetings.”

This is accountability leakage.

- “Who owned that action item?”
- “Was anyone assigned to follow up?”
- “Did that task ever make it into Asana?”
- “We talked about it, but nobody did it.”
- “The action items are buried in the transcript.”
- “I don’t know what’s still open from last week’s meeting.”
- “We need better follow-through after calls.”
- “Things fall through the cracks between meetings.”
- “The meeting creates work, but the work doesn’t get captured.”
- “We leave meetings with momentum, then lose it.”

**Marketing translation:**

Turn meeting momentum into tracked action.

---

## 8. “Risks are being noticed but not tracked.”

This is the executive / operations pain.

- “Someone flagged that risk, but where did it go?”
- “We keep identifying risks informally.”
- “We don’t have a risk register that reflects reality.”
- “The same concern comes up every month.”
- “Nobody owns mitigation.”
- “We saw this coming, but we didn’t track it.”
- “Risk lives in conversations, not systems.”
- “We need to know which risks are still open.”
- “What risks did we identify around this project?”
- “We need an early-warning system for recurring issues.”

**Marketing translation:**

Capture risks when they surface, before they become expensive.

---

## 9. “Governance is hard to keep clean.”

This is the Teal / distributed governance / nonprofit board pain.

- “Who currently holds that role?”
- “What did we decide about this policy?”
- “Was this a formal governance decision or just a conversation?”
- “Where is the latest version of the policy?”
- “We need better role clarity.”
- “Authority is unclear.”
- “The governance record is scattered.”
- “We need a clean ledger of changes.”
- “Our structure keeps evolving, but the documentation lags behind.”
- “We say we’re distributed, but the memory is centralized in a few people.”

**Marketing translation:**

Distributed teams need distributed authority and shared memory.

---

## 10. “We are drowning in tools.”

This is the zero-behavior-change pain.

- “Please don’t give us another tool.”
- “The team won’t adopt another platform.”
- “We already have too many dashboards.”
- “If it requires behavior change, it won’t happen.”
- “Nobody wants another login.”
- “We need something that works with how we already work.”
- “Can this happen automatically?”
- “The team won’t manually tag things.”
- “Adoption is always the problem.”
- “We need infrastructure, not another app.”

**Marketing translation:**

No new workflows. No new habits. No new tool for the team to remember.

Your product’s “zero behavior change” principle is one of its most important advantages: the team keeps using Google Meet, and the system captures transcripts, notes, decisions, tasks, risks, and more into structured Notion databases for review.

- “We lose strategic context between retreats.”
